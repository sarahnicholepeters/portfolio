Stagehand:

Case Study

Role: Product Designer

Tools: Figma

Overview:

Communication within the theatre is always chaos, with so much going on and with so many teams working on one project, a lot of information can get lost or overlooked. Stagehand is a web application that utilizes cloud storage to create more efficient and effective communication within the theatre.

Problem:

When you have multiple teams working on the same project, sometimes quality gets sacrificed for efficiency, leaving creative vision and promise unfulfilled. While there can only be so many meetings with cast, crew, and design teams, there needs to be a way to store share and consolidate information without bogging everyone down. That's where Stagehand comes in.

Solution:

Create a cloud storage web application that would streamline the communication between teams within the theatre. Stagehand enhances the communication experience of the production team. Information in the theatre is always changing and new ideas pop up frequently, it's hard to stay current on what the plan is. With Stagehand the user can upload, share, and edit their ideas, and keep up to the second record of what their tasks are. Along with that, they also recieve real-time updates from their stage managers and directors so that they can work with the utmost efficiency. This means you can get your work in on-time and on budget.

When creating the user survey, it was paramount to pay attention to the following:

  • Who would find this application the most helpful
  • Which apps they have used in the past
  • The feature users most desire
  • Users experience level within the theatre

The survey was posted online and was completed by over a dozen respondents. The results were varied but brought some interesting things to my attention. Users want to:

  • Create groups
  • Send updates and edits quickly and effectively
  • Upload items from their computer or internet
  • Organize content

With these results, I knew how to start building my application. It was clear to me that users wanted to have one place to go to that would store all of their information. They were tired of checking multiple accounts or losing loose paper. Putting everything in an online space would help them become more prepared and more efficient in their endeavors.

Live Clickable Prototype

View Clickable Prototype

Everything has been user-tested and thought out. The final clickable prototype took away the bells and whistles and stripped it down and wasn’t trying so hard to impress the users. It gave the users the necessary actions and worked with the users, instead of the users working for it. When it came to user testing, I sent the product to three people. I conducted two of the tests in person, and one remote. The users were all involved with the theatre at different levels, one was a student getting their MFA in theatre studies, one was an intern at a theatre in D.C, and the other was a theatre professor. Overall, the feedback from the user test was very positive. There were some notes on making images consistent and questions about why every link wasn’t active, but beyond that were more specific and insightful notes. The note that was shared amongst all participants was to add more examples of items onto each page. (i.e more groups, projects, or uploads, on their respective pages). Two users noted that the Logo was the right size, while the third said it should be smaller. As well as, making sure all hover screens are consistent.

Conclusion

When embarking on this project, I thought a web application like this would surely exist, I was surprised to find that it didn’t. After working on Stagehand for some time, I realized that one of the reasons it doesn’t is due to the idea that the theatre is a very hands-on group. Putting the items into the cloud make give some people pause. Yet, as society is moving towards a more and more paperless future, surely a place as progressive as the theatre should as well. What worked for stagehand was a simple clean design, that has color. Initially, as evidenced in the first version of the clickable prototype, I was thinking of making it more demure, dramatic, and adding too many options. When I stripped it down to the essentials and less about how to make it fell theatrical, that’s when my vision truly came to life. If given more time, I would have hand sketched more of the screens before making the prototype. I feel like ones of the walls I hit was trying to think of it on the screens online, I got too absorbed into the project. I believe that I would also talk to some professional theatres and see what they would hope for out of a website like this and what would have to happen for them to be interested.
